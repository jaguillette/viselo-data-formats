I'm Jeremy Guillette, and before we get into the fun bits of visualization, I want to take some time to talk about data formats. You won't always have us to prepare nice datasets for you, so it's worth spending some time thinking about the ways in which how your data is formatted influence what you're able to do with it.

Data as Construct

Data is created. The production of a dataset requires choicess about what and how to collect and how to encode.

There is no such thing as "raw data"

Let's get some basics out of the way. There's no such thing as "raw data" coming to us from nature. Every piece of data you find out there in the world came into existence because of decisions that were made by people about what to record and how to record it.

Organizing a Chili Recipe

Let's start with a simple example: a chili recipe.

Unstructured Data

For this recipe you will need two pounds of ground beef, a chopped half onion, a teaspoon of ground black pepper, half a teaspoon of garlic salt, and two and a half cups of tomato sauce.

First, we have the recipe as you might find it on a website, as unstructured text. The information about what goes into the recipe is there, but it's relying on the reader's ability to make sense of it.

An organized list

  • 2 pounds ground beef
  • 1/2 onion, chopped
  • 1 teaspoon ground black pepper
  • 1/2 teaspoon garlic salt
  • 2 1/2 cups tomato sauce
Now we're getting more organized, with all of the different ingredients in the recipe separated out into their own lines. This seems like a small step, but in a research project, it can be a big deal. By breaking down information into a list of pieces of the same sorts of thing, you're deciding what constitiutes an individual datum in your dataset.

"Flat" data model

Amount Unit Item State
2 lbs. ground beef -
0.5 - onion chopped
1 tsp. garlic salt -
2.5 cups tomato sauce -
Now we're getting properly structured into a spreadsheet-like data format. Spreadsheets are the most common way of structuring data, but just because you have a spreadsheet open doesn't mean you're structuring your data in a usable way. Here, we have information about the ingredients to the recipe broken down into categories. We've got the item separated from the amount, unit, and state of the ingredient. I set up this structure with making the recipe in mind, so I have items to buy at the grocery store, amounts and units to tell me how much to buy, and a state to let me know what I'll need to do with the items before they can be used. I've separated the individual things I'm interested in into rows, and decided what properties of those things I care about in this mini-dataset by putting them into columns

Wide vs. Tall Data

If you're using someone else's data, it might not come to you in a format that's ideal for visualization and analysis. Here's a common problem and some ways to deal with it.

Wide data

Year Alabama Alaska Arizona Arkansas ...
1824 49.1 ---- ---- ---- ...
1828 54.6 ---- ---- ---- ...
1832 31.5 ---- ---- ---- ...

46 rows by 56 columns

You might find a downloadable summary table of a dataset that looks like this one. It looks like a spreadsheet, but what's the actual observation represented by each row? A year that occurred, and the voter turnouts of various states during that year? In our previous example, an instance of an ingredient was what we were interested in, and that was related to the purpose of our data structure, to record information about the recipe. We had columns related to that individual piece of information. Here, the individual pieces of data are actually the turnout percentatges for a state in a year, but they're not each represented by a row. Let's see what that would look like.

Tall data

Year State Turnout
1824 Alabama 49.1
1828 Alabama 54.6
1832 Alabama 31.5
... ... ...

3 columns by 2341 rows

This is a different way of structuring the same information, and the way the data that one group today will use is actually structured. Now each row is an individual observation. We can still generate that initial view using a pivot table, but from this "taller" view, we can more easily generate visualizations, run analyses, subset the data, and enrich each observation with additional information.

Going from wide to tall

  • Often referred to as "unpivot"
  • pandas.melt() and/or pandas.wide_to_long()
  • stack() or melt() in R
Since the method for going from "tall" to "wide" data is usually a pivot table, the process of going from "wide" to "tall" data is usually referred to as "unpivoting" or "de-pivoting". In programming languages, it can also be called "melting" a dataset. Python and R have dedicated methods of making this easy, but more graphical programs can have a harder time. If you don't use a programming language in your data preparation, you can always look up "de-pivot" and the name of your preferred tool to find approaches that have worked for other folks.
